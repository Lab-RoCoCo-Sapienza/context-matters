Context Matters! Relaxing Goals with LLMs for Feasible 3D Scene Planning

IROS 2024

Emanuele Musumeci 1, Michele Brienza 1, Francesco Argenziano 1, Vincenzo Suriani 2, Daniele Nardi1, Domenico Daniele Bloisi 3,
1Sapienza University of Rome 2University of Basilicata 3International University of Rome
Paper arXiv Code
Our architecture takes as input a 3D Scene Graph [Armeni et al.] of the environment and a task expressed in natural language that may or may not be feasible. The proposed approach can provide a plan up to a certain degree of relaxation to a semantically similar task whenever the goal is not achievable as it is, and a straightforward executable plan in the cases where the goal is immediately feasible. This mimic the capability of humans to change expected outcomes on-the-go depending on the context they live in.

Abstract

Classical planning in AI and Robotics addresses complex tasks by shifting from imperative to declarative approaches (e.g., PDDL). However, these methods often fail in real scenarios due to limited robot perception and the need to ground perceptions to planning predicates. This often results in heavily hard-coded behaviors that struggle to adapt, even with scenarios where goals can be achieved through relaxed planning. Meanwhile, Large Language Models (LLMs) lead to planning systems that leverage commonsense reasoning but often at the cost of generating unfeasible and/or unsafe plans. To address these limitations, we present an approach integrating classical planning with LLMs, leveraging their ability to extract commonsense knowledge and ground actions. We propose a hierarchical formulation that enables robots to make unfeasible tasks tractable by defining functionally equivalent goals through gradual relaxation. This mechanism supports partial achievement of the intended objective, suited to the agent's specific context. Our method demonstrates its ability to adapt and execute tasks effectively within environments modeled using 3D Scene Graphs through comprehensive quantitative and qualitative evaluations. We also show how this method succeeds in complex scenarios where other benchmark methods are more likely to fail. Code, dataset, and additional material will be made available to the community.

Approach

The main contributions of this work are as follows:

  • A hierarchical formulation that leverages contextual information to divide tasks into sub-components. This enables robots to reason effectively about their environment, represent problems along two axes of feasibility and functionality, and create grounded, executable plans that outperform the current state of the art (SOTA) in handling complex tasks.
  • A bidirectional feasibility formalism to iteratively adapt tasks to be feasible within the given context. We relax the functional requirements and goal achievements to ensure practical task execution while maintaining alignment with high-level objectives.
  • We release a novel framework that integrates LLMs for reasoning and planning in an open-vocabulary setting. By incorporating contextual understanding, the framework supports robust action grounding and flexible planning across diverse and dynamic environments.
  • We publish a dataset containing everyday scenarios described through 3DSceneGraph [Armeni et al.] representations within the Gibson Environment. Our dataset includes everyday tasks specifically adapted to the robot environment, with varied difficulty levels. Ground truth data (PDDL domain, PDDL problem, and plan) for the scenarios are also generated using our architecture.
